# Stop Drawing Maps. Start Building Worlds.

Okay, real talk. How many of you have spent weeks drawing a fantasy map that you never ended up using? Yeah, I see you writers and dungeon masters. Look, I love maps, but most of the time they're just fancy ways of procrastination.

And today, I'm going to tell you exactly when you actually need one, and when you're just avoiding the real work. So, I've been world building for years now, and I've noticed something. The best fantasy worlds, whether they're in novels or at the role playing table, they don't always have maps. But when they do have them, there's always a good reason.

Let me show you what I mean. Here's an uncomfortable truth. You probably don't need a map yet. I know, I know.

I love maps, too. But hear me out. So, Keen made maps for Middle Earth, right? But why did he make them?

Because the entire story is about a journey. Frodo has to walk from the Shire all the way to Mordor. The distance matters. Geography is literally the obstacle.

Same thing at the role playinging table. If your party is doing a hex crawl or traveling across a continent, that map creates real decisions. Do we go through the mountains or around it? How many days of rations do we need?

But on the other hand, Joe Abberroy wrote the entire First Law trilogy without a map. He didn't need one. His stories were about characters, about violence and moral ambiguity. Geography was just background.

He only made a map later because fans kept asking for one. And you know what? The books worked perfectly fine without it. Think about a dungeon focused campaign.

Do you really need a continental map? No, you need a good dungeon map. But that's not the kind of map I'm talking about. So the real question isn't should I make a map?

It should be does geography actually matter to my story or campaign? Okay, so when do you need one? I've got three situations for you. Number one, your story is literally a journey.

If your characters are crossing deserts, climbing mountains, geography creates the tension. For writers, your readers need to feel how far they have to go, how exhausted they are, how dangerous the terrain is. Dungeon Masters, your players need to make meaningful choices. Which route do we take?

Do we risk the shortcut through the haunted forest or take the long way around? In Lord of the Rings, the map shows you just how impossibly far Frodo has to walk. The distance that is the despair. It is the weight of the quest made visible on the map.

At the table, it's the same thing. When your players see they're only halfway to their destination and they're already out of supplies, that's tension you probably couldn't really create without a map. Reason number two, politics and territory matter. If you've got kingdoms fighting over lands, trade routes, borders, you name it, you need to know where everything is.

Otherwise, your wars make zero sense. Game of Thrones is the perfect example. The map is gorgeous, but it's not meant to be gorgeous. It is a representation of the game board.

Every house's location explains why they do what they do. And dungeon masters, if your campaign has faction play or territorial disputes or political intrigue, your players need to understand the geography. Why is this port city so valuable? Why can't the northern kingdom just invade?

The map should answer those questions. Reason number three to have a map. You're a liar and you have a bad memory. Writers, if you wrote the mountains are to the east in chapter 2 and then west in chapter 7, yeah, you need a map.

Dungeon masters, same for you. If you told your players the capital is 3 days north, but the last session you said it was a week away. You break the illusion. The map isn't for your readers or your players.

It's to keep your lies straight because you're winging it. It's your cheat sheet so your world doesn't fall apart. But here is where everyone screws up. They make the map first.

They spend three weeks perfecting coastlines, naming the rivers, figuring out climate zones before they even know what their story or campaign is about. That's completely backwards. Your story should create the map, not the other way around, in my opinion. Writers, start with your plot.

Where do your characters go? What locations matter? What conflicts need geography to make sense? Dungeon master start with your campaign concept.

Is it a sandbox? a linear quest, political intrigue, what does this specific campaign need? And then you sketch the map. And I mean sketch, ugly, rough, whatever.

You can make it pretty later if you want or hire someone on Fiverr. But don't let making a beautiful map become the reason you never actually start. All right, so let's say you have decided you actually do need a map. Here's how you do it without wasting a month of your life.

Step one, write down your key locations. Not every village, just the places that actually appear in your story or campaign. Step two, figure out how they relate to each other. How far apart are they?

What's in between? Mountains, ocean, a cursed forest, whatever. And here's a DM tip. Think in travel time and not in miles or kilometers.

Three days on horseback is way more useful than 47 miles. Step three, sketch it out. Pencil, paper, napkin, toilet paper, I don't care, man. 5 minutes and you're done.

Step four, does it work with your story or campaign? Does this geography create the problems you need? Does it make sense for your cultures and people and travel times? If yes, cool.

You're done. If not, move some stuff around. Don't make it pretty until your story is finished or your campaign is running because I promise you it's going to change. Dungeon Masters, your players will go somewhere you didn't plan for.

Just add it to the map later. Good world building isn't about having every single detail figured out. It's about having the right details. A map is just a tool.

Sometimes you need it, sometimes you don't. The worlds that stick with readers and players are not the ones with the prettiest maps. They're the ones that feel real. It is where the geography is part of the story.

So ask yourself, is this map helping my story or my game or is it just a pretty picture?
