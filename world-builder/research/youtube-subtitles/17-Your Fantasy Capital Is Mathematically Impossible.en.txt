# Your Fantasy Capital Is Mathematically Impossible

You cannot pick how many people live in your fantasy city. Those 100,000 people you place by the river need a literal mountain of food every single day. That food has to grow somewhere. It occupies actual physical space outside those walls.

Your population size is an output. The geography goes in, the number comes out. One number before the math starts. In every pre-industrial civilization on record, between 80 and 95% of the population farms.

That number is non-negotiable. Every non-farmer in your world is a caloric debt. Every blacksmith, merchant, priest, wizard, every king, a farmer is paying that debt. The question is how many farmers are paying and how much they can actually produce.

The answer starts with the soil. An alluvial flood plain returns eight units of grain for every unit planted. The Nile, the Tigris, the Euphrates, every ancient empire that reached continental scale built its core on this type of land. That is not a coincidence.

The civilizations followed the math. Good lowland farmland in a temperate climate returns four. Rolling farmland returns three. Marginal upland soil returns only two.

Each of these land types have a different population ceiling. The simulation will run on a land at a 4:1 yield ratio. That is the optimistic end for average land. Documented medieval English yields on typical farmland ran 3:1.

The land type is the first variable your map has already decided. The base unit of this simulation is one farming family. Bob, his wife, and three children. They farm 12 hectares on a 4:1 yield ratio.

Because soil needs to rest, they only plant six hectares per year to keep those fields cycling. One adult non-farmer needs 10 buckets of grain per year to survive. That is the inland number. It assumes grain carries the entire diet.

A coastal city changes that number. Active fisheries supply roughly 20 to 30% of protein needs from the water rather than the field. The grain requirement per urban citizen drops to seven or eight buckets. The number you use depends on where your city sits.

Bob's family produces 144 buckets in a good year. That pile is a mountain. Right now it belongs to everyone except Bob. The mountain disappears in five cuts.

The state goes first. The lord does not wait for Bob. Capital tax comes off the gross harvest before anyone touches the pile. He takes 15 buckets regardless of how successful the harvest is.

The amount varies and is dependent on the base yield of lands. The church arrives second. The tithe is on first fruits, meaning the gross production. 15 more buckets gone.

30 buckets have already left the farm. Now the seed goes back into the ground. 36 buckets locked away, untouchable. The seed grain is next year's food.

Spend it now, starve to death next year. Then the barn takes its cut. A barn is a damp wooden box full of mold and rats. 20% of what remains disappears.

16 buckets gone. Bob's family ends with 62 buckets. They need 40 to survive the year. 22 buckets of surplus.

The grain is the taxable, tradeable, movable part of the harvest. The garden and the pig in the backyard are the invisible buffer for the family of Bob. The simulation just tracks the grain. Meanwhile at the lord's estate, the lord makes sure that he has all the food he needs for the coming year.

Everything else he sells on the market just like Bob. This is approximately 20% of everything he took from all the families in the area. Both of them will deal with transport losses. Not all the grain will reach the destination.

In fact, 20% will end up feeding the non-farmers in the rural area. Blacksmith, millers, you name it. If there is no navigable river within 15 miles, the grain will never leave the area. It will go to the nearest market town.

There are a few rules for losses in transport. Cart rides lose approximately 3% every 15 miles. Anything further than 15 miles is financially unviable. I call this the ox paradox.

Every handover loses about 4% to spills, spoils, theft, and other factors. Any toll station takes its share. These are your junction lord and choke point lords. The more of these the grain encounters, the higher the toll.

Sea voyage takes about 6% of the grain in transit. Undocumented routes on the Baltic, this is the percentage mentioned. And then, the destination city will take 15% famine insurance. [snorts] After all these extra cuts, whatever remains is the surplus that is available for consumption.

I have created a worksheet that goes through this process. It helps you determine whether your city's population size is realistic or if it needs some tweaking. It is available for free on my website. I will run a simulation through the worksheet right now.

Step one, mark the supply zone. For every city on my map, I marked down their supply zones. This means I draw a circle that represents a 15-mile radius around the city. If the city is on a river, I add a corridor area next to the upstream side of the river.

This stretch is 15 miles on both river banks. Now, I calculate the total amount of square miles of the area and write it down. Step two, classify the soil. Here, I look at my map and assign the soil types for each region.

I stick to one soil type in my example, but I encourage you to experiment with this. It will help you define your politically important grounds more easily. I use the table on the worksheet for writing down the yield ratio and the percentage of actually usable lands. Step three, apply the maritime modifier.

For each city, I check if there is a fish subsidiary available. If the city has access to large fisheries and salt, then the amount of buckets per urban citizen will decrease. I write down the adjusted number. Step four, calculate the agricultural footprint.

As we already saw in the table of step two, not all the lands are actually fit for agriculture. Here I calculate the agricultural footprint of each zone in hectares. This number is pivotal. Step five, apply technology modifiers.

This is an optional step. Perhaps your world is a little more advanced than the standard medieval setting, but not quite industrial yet. Look at the table and write down what it means for you. Step six, count the families.

We want to know how many farming families there are and how that translates to population. We use a combination of numbers we have previously defined and out rolls the number. Step seven, choose your rotation system. This step is quite important.

It can mean the difference between a very large city and a modest town. It determines how much of the plot size, so the total land available to Bob, is actually in use every year. A fully utilized plot is twice as productive as a half used plot. Imagine the difference of the grain mountain.

Step eight, calculate your surplus grain. In this step, we calculate the surplus grain. In short, before the grain heads off to the city, different parties take their share. Whatever ends up as surplus, that is going to be sent over to the city.

There's lots of calculations on this page. If you struggle in this part, feel free to send me a message on Discord. The server link is in the description. Step nine, the grain's journey.

The last part. Here we follow all the surplus grain to the destination city. Along the way we will pass points where parts of the grain mountain will disappear. Sometimes it's spillage, theft, taxes, or even famine insurance.

Once we went through all of it, out rolls a number. And that number is the absolute maximum of your city. Unless of course you decide your city is an empire's capital which parasites all the other people. That should have major implications for your world, and that's on you.
