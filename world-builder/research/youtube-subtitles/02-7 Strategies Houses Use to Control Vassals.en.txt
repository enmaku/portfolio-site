# 7 Strategies Houses Use to Control Vassals

The vassels in your fantasy world are loyal today, but loyalty decays. Last time we built the vassel web. We mapped the three layers of politics and we figured out why vassels are loyal in the first place. But I didn't show you the hardest part, keeping them loyal.

Don't treat loyalty like something permanent. It's not. In real long-term politics, loyalty is a process. It needs constant maintenance.

When you try to write long-term politics, the loyalty feels static, frozen. Here's what I realized after building dozens of these systems. Loyalty isn't a switch. It's not on or off.

Look at it like a fire. And fires need fuel to keep the flame alive. If you want your politics to feel alive, you need to understand the maintenance game because every house is playing it constantly. Let me show you the seven mechanisms to maintain loyalty.

And here's the key. Every mechanism also creates a vulnerability. Mechanism one, rewards. You give them something, land, titles, tax breaks.

Simple, right? But here's the problem. Rewards create expectations. If you reward vessel A, vessel B feels slighted.

And if you stop rewarding, they will see you as weak. Mechanism two, marriages. You bind houses through blood. Your daughter marries their heir.

You are now bound by blood. But here's the crack. Marriages create competing loyalties. When your vassel's son is married to not your but your rivals daughter, where does his loyalty actually lie?

And sort of a bonus mechanism is the darker version of this very tool. You take their children as wards, send your son to be raised in my court. It's protection and it's education. It's also a polite hostage situation.

Mechanism three, military dependence. You make them need you for protection. You station troops on their border. You defend them.

You crush their rivals for them. But here's the trap. If you fail to protect them even once, the loyalty starts crumbling. Military dependence only works if you're consistently strong.

And on the flip side of this mechanism, if you're too strong, they start to fear you instead of their enemies. Fear can keep them loyal, but fear also makes them look for exits. Mechanism four, economic control. You make them dependent on economic benefits you provide.

You grant them exclusive trade rights. You give them access to your ports and markets. You build roads through their land that increase their wealth. But be careful.

You didn't just build a trade route. You build a highway for your enemies as well. If a rival house offers them better access or better terms, they will listen. And if you revoke their privilege or give the same deal to their rival, they will feel betrayed.

Economic loyalty only lasts as long as you're their best option. Mechanism five, shared enemies. You unite them against a common threat, an external kingdom, a rival great house, bandits, monsters. But here's the problem.

Shared enemies only work while the enemy exists. If you defeat the enemy, the very reason for their loyalty no longer exists. And if the enemy becomes too strong, your vassels might switch sides just to survive. Shared enemies are the most powerful short-term glue, but they're terrible for long-term loyalty.

Mechanism six, oaths and tradition. You lean on history. Your grandfather swore to mine. We've been allies for 300 years.

Public ceremonies, oath renewals, shaming those who break tradition. But the weakness is really this. Tradition only works if people still believe in it. If your house dishonors the tradition first, the oath means nothing.

And younger generations care less about their grandfather's promises. Oaths are strongest in stable times. In chaos, they're just words. Mechanism seven, secrets.

You know things about them they don't want revealed. Proof of a crime, a bastard child. But here's the danger. Blackmail creates enemies, not allies.

The moment they think you'll expose them anyway, they have nothing to lose. Secrets are the most dangerous tool because once you use them, you can never take them back. Here is where it gets interesting. You cannot just use one mechanism.

In the meantime, Draor panics. He knows that Kale is a flight risk, so he threatens to reveal that Kale's lord is a kinslayer. The blackmail activates. An enemy is created and now that marriage, it stops being a bond and starts to become a hostage situation.

Kale's heir is trapped. Kale cannot openly rebel without risking his son's life. So they do what many vassels do. They fund independence movements in secret.

Loyal in name only. One naval battle and all the layered mechanism start to fail. And that is how loyalty really collapses. Maintaining loyalty is brutally expensive.

It costs wealth, children, soldiers, economic flexibility, and that's just for one vassel. You have multiple vassels. They all need maintenance, but you cannot afford to maintain everyone equally. So, you have to choose favorites.

And the moment you do that, the other houses start drifting. This is why houses fall. Not just because they're weak per se, but because they run out of tools to keep everyone loyal. So, what exactly happens when maintenance fails?

There's three outcomes really.
