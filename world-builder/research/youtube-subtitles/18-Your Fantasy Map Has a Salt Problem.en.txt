# Your Fantasy Map Has a Salt Problem

Bob the farmer slaughters his pig in autumn. By winter that same pig could feed his family for weeks. Instead, the pig will turn green by the end of the week. Food has an expiration date and nature does not care about how badly you need it.

In the pre-modern world, every layer of society faced the same problem. A farmer has to feed a family. A king has to feed an army on the march and a fleet between harbors. The further you reach, the faster your food betrays you.

There aren't any freezers, no vacuum seals or chemical preservatives to stop it. Smoking buys some time, drying buys some time, putting it in cold cellars buys some time. But time is always running out. But not with salt.

Salt stops the clock. Preserve the food and you preserve your civilization. Salt is a world-building goldmine or salt mine. Salt does one thing extremely well and it does it to almost everything.

It pulls the water out of food and rot needs water to live. The pig that the farmer killed in autumn now lasts the winter. The body cannot run without salt. Everyone needs salt.

Because the food lasts longer, armies can march further. Sailors can venture out to distant shores that would otherwise be impossible to reach on bread and smoked salmon alone. If food cannot be preserved, your people cannot go far without starving. Salt is what converts perishable proteins into movable calories and this creates enormous value.

Salt used to be so valuable that it was traded for gold. A slab of salt spent like a coin. That's where phrases such as he is worth his salt come from. Salary literally means salt payment.

Salt comes from three places and the first is the easiest. Where the coast is hot and dry, the sea will make salt for free. Rob owns that kind of land. His workers dig basins into the ground where the sea water flows in with every tide.

The water cannot return to the ocean and is now trapped. The sun beats down on each basin until the water evaporates and only salt is left. Solar evaporation. For thousands of years, people have used this method to produce salt.

In ancient Mesopotamia, the desert sun and dry winds helped evaporate brine-filled basins. A reliable means of production. Every civilization needs salt. Salt travels from the coast along roads inland.

And this is what distant communities and lets them grow far larger than they otherwise could. A community that depends on imported salt will defend the road that brings it. They maintain it, guard it, and build their towns alongside it. Rome built the Via Salaria for exactly this.

Deep inland there is no coast to evaporate, but sometimes the ground leaks salt water of its own, a brine spring. Bob lives out there, so Bob boils it. This means he needs firewood. All the firewood he uses to boil the brine water cannot directly be used to warm his house.

So there has to be a balance. If Bob uses all the wood to produce salt, he may freeze to death the next winter. It might not be as much as the sea, but it would still provide enough. The third source is the oldest.

Some land sits on a sea that dried up before history began, leaving the salt buried in the rocks. Bob digs and Bob finds a mine. Now that Bob has found his salt mine, he hires some workers to mine it and transport it. Some of it goes to the nearest city, some of it goes along the river to distant cities where salt is scarce.

In those places Bob can sell it for a much higher price. Demand creates value. Some cities would become so closely associated with the salt trade that it can even be found in their name. For example, Hall in Hallstatt derives from an old word for salt, Hallstatt, place of salt.

Salzburg also refers to salt. Bob then invests in ships that sell his salt to distant coasts with different climates, places that cannot easily produce their own salt. They have fish, herring, game from the forests, but without salt they struggle to preserve it all. So they buy from Bob.

They salt the fish, store the meat, and prepare for the coming harsh winter. This kind of salt trade is part of what made the Hanseatic League so wealthy. They provided most of northern Europe with the means to preserve food. This wealth paid for their own fleets and their own politics, a trading block that answered to nobody.

Funded by fish that would have rotted without salt. The three salt sources feed a trade network and change the whole economy. Salt traders become extremely wealthy. The local lord notices that suddenly Bob can spend almost more money than he can.

Bob enjoys the finest food and the finest clothes. He doesn't really care about Bob, but he does care about his salt mine. So, he taxes Bob for mining salt in his lands. In return, he also gives Bob a license to rightfully mine it.

This provides the local lord with a stable income from Bob's labor while allowing Bob to keep mining. Bob gains more and more influence. He now controls where the salt goes, who gets it and who does not. The only person he pays is the lord.

Unlike grain, which you can plant and grow, you cannot grow salt. You cannot just find it everywhere. This begins to bother other nobles. Even the king does not like it.

So, the king passes a salt tax. Now, everyone has to pay. In places with a lot of salt, the taxes are very high. The tax for Bob is immense.

Bob loses a lot of his money and decides to look for an alternative. Maybe he can bribe an official to falsify tax records for him. Maybe he falsifies his own. Or he hires smugglers that avoid checkpoints altogether.

In France, the salt tax or gabelle became so unpopular that it led to an entire class of illegal salt traders known as the faux saunier. They bought salt from a place where it was cheap and sold it in places where the taxes were high. Which means the real power belongs to those who command the trade routes. Bob, the lord, the smugglers, and the king each take a piece.

It isn't much of a choice because everyone needs salt. A single scarce resource can bend trade routes, raise cities, start wars, and decide who rises and who starves. Salt already did all of that. To help you explore the concept, I've created a worksheet consisting of six steps.

By the end, you will know where your salt comes from, who controls it, who profits, and what happens when a supply stop. And you can even adapt the worksheet to whichever resource you need it for. You can download it for free on my website or on my Discord. Links in the description.

You will be able to create opportunities for wealth, tension, politics, and all kinds of stories. After all, every world needs its salt, and every Bob deserves a chance to get rich. Step one, mark the sources. First, mark where your map there are coasts with a hot and dry climate.

These will give you sea pans that allow for solar evaporation. More inland, you will need some rock salt deposit or possibly brine springs. These will function as alternative salt resources. If your realm doesn't have any of these, it is considered salt starved and depends entirely on import.

Step two, salt starved regions. When you have salt sources, you will also need somewhere to supply it to. Salt starved regions. These are places that do not have a nearby source of salt.

They rely heavily on import. Usually, they are far inland and just one embargo away from a crisis. That alone gives you enough tension for conflict on the map. If they don't obey the salt lord, they could die.

Step three, the salt lord. Now, you need someone to own this source node, a salt lord. Ask yourself who controls this source. Does the king control it, or does a noble from a some great house control it?

If it's a noble, then his wealth may even rival that of the king himself, because salt is so economically rich. Step four, salt [snorts] roads. Once you have the salt sources, you will be needing trade routes. These are the arteries of your kingdom.

They will provide cities and regions with salt. That means they have to be well defended. You need to mark choke points that allow for a toll by lords that let you pass through their lands. Salt is worth the distance because the orcs will not eat it like it would eat grain.

Step five, the salt tax. Now, you have a salt source, a salt lord, and places to transport it to. The next step is applying a salt tax. You need to choose who collects it.

Is it the crown? Is it a noble house? A group of bandits that you need to pay off to pass through their forest? More than that, you also need to decide how high the tax is going to be.

If it is too high, then people will be looking for ways to get it as cheap as possible. This allows for smuggler economies. Those same bandits can take bags of salt and distribute it across the realm. Otherwise, the people will not be happy with a high tax.

You also need to determine who pays most. Only the salt lord or all the lords? If some pay more than others, you will breed resentment. Step six, hook for conflict or story.

The final part, creating a story hook. Based on all of the previous steps, you should have enough tension somewhere to start your journey. Armies can be grounded because supply routes are disrupted, smuggler networks can act as shadow powers that undermine the social economical landscape. Everyone can fight a holy war over who gets to be the salt lord.
